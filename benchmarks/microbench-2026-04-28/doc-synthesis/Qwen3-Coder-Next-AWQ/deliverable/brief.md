# Executive Brief: Nimbus Logistics, Inc. — Follow-on Investment Recommendation

**Recommendation: Pass** — unless the investor can verify that net retention is truly above 110%, the adjusted ARR ($36–37M) is stable and growing, and the three new $1M+ ARR contracts are fully signed and not at risk of slippage.

---

## The Company’s Story

Nimbus Logistics, a cloud-native logistics platform for mid-market e-commerce brands, closed an $84M Series B at a $560M post-money valuation in January 2026, led by Lightning Capital with participation from Sequoia, Accel, and FedEx Ventures [Source 1]. The company reported $42M in ARR as of Q4 2025, up from $11M a year prior, and serves over 1,400 customers with 187 employees across Oakland, Austin, and Berlin [Source 1].

---

## The Reality Check

The company’s public narrative masks significant operational strain. According to TechCrunch, two of Nimbus’s top-five customers — including Sundry Brands, a $4.2M ARR contract — did not renew in late 2025, and a second top-five customer, Klatch, exited in December [Source 2]. Adjusted for this December churn, the current run-rate ARR is closer to $36–37M, not $42M [Source 2].

The Series B was originally intended to close at a $720M valuation in late 2025 but was repriced down after the Sundry non-renewal became known [Source 2]. This suggests the market, not just the company, recognized the headwinds.

An internal memo dated February 28, 2026, outlines a sharp pivot: a 30% reduction in international headcount (Berlin: 41 → ~28–30), a pause on EMEA hiring, and the sunset of the “Nimbus Lite” tier (310 customers, $5K–$20K ARR each) [Source 4]. This directly contradicts the press release’s promise of “accelerated international expansion” [Source 1].

---

## Key Risks

**Customer concentration and churn.** The top five customers represented ~38% of ARR, and two have already left [Source 3]. The company’s claim of “net retention above 110%” [Source 2] is inconsistent with this level of churn unless offset by massive new logo growth — and the credibility of that claim hinges on the three new $1M+ ARR contracts announced in Q1 2026 [Source 2]. Without verification, this is a red flag.

**High burn and layoffs.** According to a former senior engineer, the company was burning ~$4M/month all-in against ~$3.5M/month in revenue in mid-2025 [Source 3]. This unsustainable model led to quiet layoffs of 22 people (11% of the org) in February 2025, mostly in customer success and business development [Source 3].

**Runway concerns.** While the company secured a $50M credit facility and extended runway to 36+ months [Source 4], the memo frames this as “insurance, not operating capital” — a clear signal that the current path is not viable [Source 4].

---

## Conditions for Reconsideration

The investor should pass unless they can independently verify:

1. **Net retention is truly above 110%** — not just new logos masking churn.
2. **The adjusted ARR ($36–37M) is stable and growing** — not declining further.
3. **The three new $1M+ ARR contracts are fully signed and not at risk of slippage** — not just term sheets.

If these conditions are met, the company’s engineering quality (described as “genuinely good” by a former senior engineer) [Source 3] and strong customer love in the mid-market segment [Source 3] could support a turnaround. But absent that verification, the current state does not justify a follow-on investment.

---

## Final Recommendation

**Pass.** The company’s aggressive growth narrative is contradicted by operational reality: high burn, customer churn, international retreat, and a repriced round. The $84M raise and $560M valuation look impressive on paper, but the internal memo and TechCrunch reporting reveal a company in defensive mode. The investor should wait for clearer evidence of sustainable growth before committing follow-on capital.
